#1. TITLE
─────────
My project is called Explorentory — a rental discovery platform for New York City
that uses machine learning, AI, and geospatial visualization to help people find
where they want to live. The key word is "discover." Not filter, not search —
discover.


#2. RESEARCH QUESTIONS
───────────────────────
The project is organized around three research questions. How can AI translate
natural language preferences into personalized recommendations? How can machine
learning adapt based on user behavior and feedback? And how can interactive
visualization make spatial trade-offs legible?

These aren't purely technical questions. They're design questions — about how
people make complex decisions with machine assistance.


#3. PLATFORM COMPARISON
────────────────────────
To understand why this matters, look at how people search for apartments today.
Platforms like Zillow ask you to commit before you can even see what's out there.
You set a hard budget, a fixed bedroom count, a specific neighborhood — and then
you get a list and check each one manually.

This works. But it has a hidden cost: you never see what you almost found.
A great apartment $100 over your budget is invisible to you. You can't express
"I care more about space than location" and have the platform reason about that.
You can't compare trade-offs across properties. The whole process puts the
cognitive burden entirely on you.

Explorentory flips the model. We surface a wide candidate set first, and then
use your stated priorities and your actual ratings to rank and narrow —
letting you explore before you commit.


#4 & 5. ARCHITECTURE AND USER FLOW
────────────────────────────────────
The system is a straightforward three-tier architecture: a PostGIS database
of 4 million synthetic NYC units, a FastAPI backend running the ML pipeline
and the OpenAI API, and a vanilla JavaScript frontend with MapLibre for maps
and canvas-based charts.

The user experience unfolds in four stages, which you can see in these screenshots:
initial preference input, clicking a neighborhood directly on the map, a ten-property
rating survey, and finally the results interface — where the real exploration begins.


#6. SCORING PIPELINE
─────────────────────
The scoring is where the machine learning actually happens.

We start with 4 million units. Rule-based filtering — rent tolerance,
bedroom and bathroom range — cuts that down to around 100,000 candidates.
Then a hybrid scoring model runs: 50% rule-based preference weights,
50% OLS regression trained on your 10 ratings, using 11 engineered features.
That produces 5,000 ranked results.

From there, it's the user's turn. You can chat in natural language, filter
spatially on the map, pull up AI-generated explanations for individual properties,
and compare trade-offs visually with radar charts and histograms.

#7. CLOSING
────────────
Let me close with what I think this project is really pointing at.

For most of computing history, software was exceptional at one thing:
executing precise instructions at scale. Give it a rule — it follows the rule.
Give it a filter — it filters.

Machine learning gave us the next shift: systems that could learn from data,
adapt to patterns, personalize — still quantitative, but now responsive.

Generative AI is a third shift. And it is different in kind, not just degree.
For the first time, machines can work with input that is ambiguous, subjective,
even contradictory — the kind of input that used to be exclusively human territory.

"I want somewhere cozy, close to good coffee, not too loud on weekends."
That is not a filter. That is a feeling.
And now, a machine can reason about it.

Rental search is a small, contained version of a much larger question.
Across healthcare, education, urban planning, hiring — we are beginning to
delegate decisions involving human ambiguity to systems that can learn from it.

The designer's role in this world is not simply to make interfaces cleaner
or instructions simpler. It is to design the boundary between human uncertainty
and machine intelligence. To make ambiguity legible. To make trade-offs visible.
To build systems where people understand not just what the machine decided —
but why, and whether they trust it.

That question is not going away. If anything, it becomes more urgent every year.

Explorentory is a small answer. But the question it is asking is not small.
